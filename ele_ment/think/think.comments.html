Better check those dates as far as "predating Juan" goes.
I should check them before writing :) but if you believe
the Detroit Free Press Magazine article of Feb. 4, 1990:

     "Davis was a Vietnam Veteran with world experience under his belt,
     Atkins an eager apprentice. The two became Cybotron and recorded
     their first hit, a funky, dance tune called 'Cosmic Car,' in the
     late '70s on their own label, Deep Space Records.'

One of these days, someone has to sit down with Juan, Derrick, and Kevin
and nail down all their early influences. Get the timeline straight.

I think Cybotron's "Alleys of your mind" sold 15,000 copies in '81,
and owes a debt to Ultravox's "Mr. X" of 1980. Which in turn owes
a debt to Kraftwerk's "Hall of Mirrors" from the album Trans Europe Express.

Another infulence of Detroit Techno is Gary Numan, and his song "Cars" ('79?)

An interesting note: since the '60s, the "Detroit Sound" has been
compared to a relentless driving assembly-line sound. A main
characteristic of early Motown was it's lack of a bridge--the music
doesn't stop.

Back to techno in general:
You really can't say enough about Kraftwerk, whose 1974 album "Autobahn"
influenced techno, hip hop, new wave, techno-pop, progressive rock, and
most of the second side sounds pretty ambient. :)

If I had to pick the oldest record that sounds like the newest techno, it
would have to be "The Wozard of Iz" by Bernard Krause, circa 1970, which
has several bars of modular moog riffing that could be taken for 808 State.

While more industrial than techno, George Antheil certainly deserves
a mention as a mid-20th century composer. And the original Art of Noises
came from Italian Futurist Marinetti in the early 20th cen. For that matter,
where's the influence of film, literature, Art, the 1939 world's fair, and the
industrial age itself? Techno didn't occur in a vaccuum. :)

BTW, the word "techno" was first used in the 1800s, according to the OED.

Apart from this, check the alt.rave FAQ (it's on hyperreal.com)
