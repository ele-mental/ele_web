J A C Q U E S   D E R R I D A
A Meta-Critical Examination of Three Texts
and Some Comments on Their Relevance to Dance

Edward Luna
Dance Aesthetics & Criticism
The Ohio State University
Instructor: Candace Feck
AU 2001


Jacques Derrida is among the most quoted philosophical thinkers to emerge in the last thirty years. His vast body of work -- clear and well-articulated in its intention but opaque and eruditic in its style -- has been eagerly taken up by the fields of architecture, cultural criticism, media studies, literary criticism, anthropology, the philosophy of science, performance studies, film studies, and various other disciplines -- all claiming post-structuralism as worthy of investigation.

Yet his relevance to dance has scarcely been traced. This brief essay attempts to address the little-explored convergence between Derrida's thought and the discipline of dance, by an examination of some of his basic contributions to our current cultural and philosophical climate. I am drawing primarily from excerpts from two of his seminal texts: "Structure, Sign, and Play in the Discourse of the Human Sciences" (from Contemporary Literary Criticism, 2nd edition. Robert Con Davis and Ronald Schleifer, editors; Longman, New York, 1989); and "Différance" (from Speech and Phenomena, and Other Essays on Husserl's Theory of Signs, translated by David B. Allison; Evanston IL, Northwestern University Press, 1973); plus a third source about Derrida by Christopher Johnson, simply entitled Derrida (The Great Philosophers, consulting editors Ray Monk and Frederic Raphael; Phoenix, London, 1998), subtitled The Scene of Writing, examining Derrida's 1967 book Of Grammatology (translated by Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, Baltimore, The Johns Hopkins University Press, 1976).

Derrida's work tends to coalesce around a series of concerns: namely, those of identity, writing, interpretation, signification, deconstruction, and différance. I am concentrating in this paper on the concept of différance and its many implications, which will reveal something about his other concerns in the process.

Différance is a peculiar play on words which is untranslatable in English. It takes on the meaning of the English word "difference" but adds to it an important temporal sense of "deferral," which, as Derrida himself says, "puts off until 'later' what is presently denied."1 Différance is "neither a word nor a concept"2 but rather a strategy for understanding, or rather, revealing that which is imperceptible. Derrida sets this non-word, non-concept to the task of articulating, and extending in a radical way, Saussure's understanding of language as a system of differences "without positive terms." Saussure, it should be recalled, contributed to the foundation of structuralism with his notion that language is a system, "in which it is the relations between elements and not the elements themselves that are responsible for meaning"3. In short, différance is Derrida's way of expanding Saussure's theory to its logical end:
  The first consequence to be drawn from this is that the signified concept is never present to itself, in an adequate presence that would only refer to itself. Every concept is necessarily and essentially inscribed in a chain or a system, within which it refers to another and to other concepts, by the systematic play of differences. Such a play, then -- différance -- is no longer simply a concept, but the possibility of conceptuality, of the conceptual system and process in general.4

Thus, if concepts are chained so resolutely as Derrida claims, no concept exists which is not (always) already part of a chain. In other words (and this is much in line with structuralist reasoning) there is no possibility of a concept existing outside the system; it is always (already) part of a system of differentiation which both defines it and fails to define it at the same time. This is where différance "exists," if indeed it can be said to "exist" at all -- not at the center, nor at the not-center, but at the place of différance itself.

But Derrida is not content to merely overturn one of the founding tenets of western philosophy -- the self-evidence of the origin, and self-presence (outlined by Aristotle and Bertrand Russell, among others)5 -- for he goes on to immediately reinstate it, in a completely different way:
  What we note as différance will thus be the movement of play that "produces" (and not by something that is simply an activity) these differences, these effects of difference… Différance is the nonfull, nonsimple "origin"; it is the structured and differing origin of differences.6

The relevance of this concept to he field of dance scholarship will merely be alluded to here, but it is enough to ask whether or not iterations of the body, grounded in particular vocabularies of movement, have suffered from a similarly short-sighted understanding of presence and self-evidence -- performance is clearly not evident, if it depends on other bodies (or viewers) to prove its existence, which in turn depends on other bodies (or viewers), and so on ad infinitum. Yet, at the same time, dance seems to instinctively draw from the productive possibilities of playing with the spatial and temporal thereness of a body (i.e., its presence), while simultaneously celebrating its non-presence, or the immediate disappearance of a performance after it has traced its lines through space and time. [This will be elaborated more fully below.]

Here it is worth considering Derrida's notion of inaudibility, where "the a of différance…is not heard; it remains silent, secret, and discreet, like a tomb." In this case, Derrida moves to an examination of graphical manifestations of language, i. e., writing. He argues that the distinction between "différence" and "différance" can only be made through writing; in French, the distinction is inaudible. Moreover, Derrida tells us that
  this silence that functions only within what is called phonetic writing…points out or reminds us in a very opportune way that, contrary to an enormous prejudice, there is no phonetic writing… The difference that brings out phonemes and lets them be heard and understood [entendre] itself remains inaudible.7

To summarize this rather surprising statement, the difference between "différence" and "différance" cannot be accounted for audibly, yet it exists nonetheless. If that is the case, then the claim that writing is merely a recording of speech -- the supposed originary mode of communication -- is incorrect. Writing therefore, has a primacy that has been heretofore unacknowledged, which leads Derrida to conclude that the distinction between writing, a graphic representation, and speech, an audible flow, is not as clear as most philosophical inquiry has long assumed. Among the many implications Derrida draws out from this is an expansion of the very notion of writing. For Derrida, as Christopher Johnson summarizes,

  Writing in the general sense of the term extends beyond even the widest definition of empirical systems of writing; rather it is the condition of possibility of writing in the common sense of the term, and indeed of language in general.8

Expanding this idea even farther, Johnson states that
  From this structural and historical perspective, the question of where and when writing begins…becomes superfluous, or at best, relative. Derrida's conception of writing defamiliarizes the customary distinctions made between speech and writing, life and death, presence, and absence, the human and the animal, the human and the technological, and emphasizes instead their necessary co-implication and continuity.9

In this light, let us take another look at the defamiliarizing, decentering effects generated by différance, in relation to dance. In "Structure, Sign, and Play," Derrida begins to defamiliarize us with the center in terms of "substitution":
  The substitute does not substitute itself for anything which has somehow existed before it. Henceforth, it was necessary to begin thinking that there was no center, that the center could not be thought in the form of a present-being, that the center had no natural site, that it was not a fixed locus but a function, a sort of nonlocus in which an infinite number of sign-substitutions came into play.10

At the risk of misappropriating Derrida's very specific (and highly context-bound) argument, let us consider what this substitution might mean in terms of the performing body. In dance performance, the dancer often takes on a kind of "character" (or "character of movement"), substituting what is presumably a self-present consciousness with a kind of "non-consciousness" that takes over and motivates movement. Yet if this character is merely a replacement for a present performer, or if the performer somehow has to set aside a "true self" in the act of taking on this character or choreography, it implies that the "true self" has, within itself, the condition of possibility of untruth, and therefore of some degree of replaceability. So again, in affirming the opposition between presence and non-presence, we find that the distinction in not as clear as we once thought (this defamiliarization/destabilization is, in effect, deconstruction at work).

Here, I am tempted to exaggerate the implication that a performer engaged in a performance might not thus be the "center," a moving body in space, and is, instead, merely a "function," or "nonlocus" ("nonpresence"). Despite the apparent extremity of this view, dance scholar (and Frankfurt Ballet collaborator) Heidi Gilpin might agree:
  We can never know what took place [in a performance], because the image etched in memory is transformed the moment we attempt o reexamine it. Performance, through its embodiment of absence, in is enactment of disappearance, can only leave traces for us to search between, among, beyond.11

The "loss" of performance, or its immediate disappearance as it enters our awareness, is similar to Derrida's comments on "the absent origin," of différance, but he also goes on to say that there is indeed more than just "the saddened, negative, nostalgic, guilty, Rousseauistic side of the thinking"12 -- indeed, there exists
  the joyous affirmation of the play of the world and of the innocence of becoming, the affirmation of a world of signs without fault, without truth, and without origin which is offered to an active interpretation.13

Moreover, if we are doomed to "interpret" the moment we perceive a dance, we are at least offered two kinds of interpretation: one, which "dreams of deciphering a truth or an origin which escapes play and the order of the sign," and the other, which "is no longer turned toward the origin, affirms play and tries to pass beyond man and humanism…" Navigating between these opposite modes, Derrida suggests, is not so much a matter of choice as it is a way to "conceive of the common ground, and the differance of this irreducible difference."14

In other words, as viewers and interpreters of dance, we must not be paralyzed by our inability to capture the essence of a performance that has just happened before our very eyes. Instead, we must acknowledge the irreconcilable but equally valid truths embedded in différance; that even as we interpret a dance, we are closing off and denying possible avenues of interpretation, but at the same time we are also generating and affirming new interpretations that may expand the scope of interpretation, and dance in general.

In closing this (all to brief) essay, I would like to call attention to a final suggestion from Derrida, where he asks that we not turn our eyes away
  when faced by the as yet unnamable which is proclaiming itself and which can do so, as is necessary whenever a birth is in the offing, only under the species of the nonspecies, in the formless, mute, infant, and terrifying form of monstrosity.15
